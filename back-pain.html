Back Pain

Back pain is a common complaint. 80% of us will have back pain at some point in our lives. Mostly back pain is nothing to worry about and will pass in a number of weeks. This doesn't stop it being painful, annoying, frustrating and worrisome.

If you cut your finger you can see it, you have probably cut yourself before, you know what is going to happen. It hurts, but you are not worried, you might have to avoid doing some things for a bit. You watch it heal. Before it is fully healed it stops hurting. You forget and try doing up a button and remember again. You do up the button in an awkward manner for a few weeks. All is normal.

If you hurt your back, you can't see it. It might not have happened before. Suddenly you can't do something that seems fundamental like bending over. You take the day off work. You worry. Will I ever be able to put my socks on without pain again? You can't see it heal. Mostly before it is fully healed the pain goes away. You forget about it. One morning you are putting your socks on in a rush and remember again. You put your socks on slowly for a while. This is normal, but sometimes it doesn't work this way and you may need some advice, reassurance or treatment to help resolve the problem.

Below I have answered a few questions you may have. If in doubt seek expert advice. Here at Reability we are always ready to listen to you. We can help understand where you are at the moment and how to help you proceed with your life especially if the pain has been with you for too long.

Common questions about back pain

Is my back pain cancer

A small number, < 1%, of people who have back pain have a definite pathological cause of their back pain. For example a vertebral fracture, malignancy, or infection1 Cancer is the smallest fraction of that proportion. If you have metastatic cancer or a past history of lung, breast, prostate, gastrointestinal or thyroid cancer it increases your chances of the back pain you are feeling being caused by cancer.

Although a number of cancers do not have any signs or symptoms. Some indications that you could have cancer include feeling unusually tired, loss of weight, loss of appetite, feeling nauseous or vomiting.

If you are feeling any of these symptoms you should consult a specialist, especially if the symptoms have been getting worse over a period of time.

Is back pain my kidneys

Your kidneys are quite high up on either side of your back, protected by the lower couple of ribs and a layer of fat. They help filter your blood and regulate your blood pressure.

Kidney infections are quite rare 2-4 people per 10,000 people each year in one study2. The pain normally won't be affected by the position you are in or who you move. You could have a kidney infection if you have:

  • One sided pain that is dull and constant
  • A recent urinary tract infection.
  • Aches in your body, a temperature and tiredness

Kidney stones are when a solid piece of material, most often a calcium salt, forms in the kidney. These can start to cause pain as they move out of the kidney. This pain is described as more of a sharp pain and will most likely be one sided. About 3.5% of people in Scotland have kidney stones.3 One of the 2018 Ig nobel prizes was for research into whether roller coasters could aid in the passing of kidney stones4,, but it is not accepted medical practice.

Is my back pain a muscle or disc

We don't know. Really. Hear me out.

There are many definitions of pain. One recent one is that

â€œPain is a distressing experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage with sensory, emotional, cognitive and social components"

Another one I like is "Pain is whatever the experiencing person says it is, existing whenever the experiencing person say it does"

One of the implications of this is that pain does not mean that there is necessarily and damage to a tissue in the body. There are receptors in the tissues, called nociceptors, that are part of the danger signalling system of the body. These along with our thoughts, beliefs, how well we are rested, how stressed we are and lots of other factors go into our central nervous system which decides how to respond. If the central nervous system decides there is potential danger one of the outputs could be pain. Other outputs could be to do with our immune system or simply moving out of danger. When there is a physical accident, tissue damage can cause pain, and it could be a muscle or a disc or any of the other tissues in the back that have nociceptors. If the pain has come on for no apparent reason, other factors could be contributing to the pain. This can be frustrating as a patient as we like to know what is wrong and a pulled muscle is easier to grasp than something like, you haven't been sleeping, you are worried about your mother, stressed at your job, you've inherited your aunts back, combined with sitting funny at a meeting the other day and just getting over a cold all of which have made you more prone to feeling pain and any one of those factors could tip you 'over the line' into feeling pain.

Do I need a new mattress If you daily wake up in pain and your symptoms slowly recover during the day. It might be good to consider your sleeping arrangements. Especially if you feel relief when you are on holiday or visiting friends. Although having holidays and being with people you love generally has a positive effect on how we experience pain (see above) so try not to expect a single external factor to solve all your issues.
Do I need an MRI for my back pain

Humans are really clever and we look for patterns and causes whenever we can. We are developing better and better ways to see smaller and more detailed parts of the world and our bodies. When MRI was first developed only people in severe pain were scanned as it was an expensive procedure. When we looked at the images we found a number of things that we thought could be causing the pain. Disc degeneration, bulges and protrusions or facet joint degeneration. As time has gone on and more people who do not have symptoms have been scanned we have discovered that these 'findings' are not the diagnostic miracle we at first thought. Why is my back pain worse at night

There are a number of reasons your back pain may be worse at night. It may be that when you are lying down the shift in gravity is pulling on tissues around your spine differently aggravating your symptoms or you are putting pressure on an area that is sensitive.

Sometimes it is just that nothing else is going on and so your focus is drawn towards the pain and it takes up most of your attention.

There are a few serious reasons why you might have back pain at night. Spinal tumours have been known to cause night pain. Spinal tumours are rare, the biggest risk factor is if you have previously had cancer. Ankylosing spondylitis can affect the joints in your lower back and pelvis and often causes night pain. Osteomyelitis (a type of bone infection) can be worse at night. This is normally accompanied by a high temperature as well as local swelling and redness. If you think you might have any of these seek medical advice.

Is my back pain Ankylosing spondylitis Ankylosing spondylitis the words are derived from Greek and relate to hardening or immobility of the spine together with inflammation. It is one of several associated conditions that are known collectively as spondyloarthritis. If you are under about 35, the pain has come on slowly, you get no relief from rest or exercise or you get back pain at night that is better when if you get up and move around you may have one of the forms of spondyloarthritis. Other symptoms could be psoriasis, finger swelling, eye inflammation, tendon issues or gut problems. Often the pain responds to ibuprofen, naproxin, diclofenic or other Non-steroidal anti inflammatory drug (NSAID).
Back pain during my Period

People often feel back pain during their period. The uterus isn't in the back, but some of the nerves that supply it go to the spine. As all nerve impulses are electrical signals when the signals reach the spine sometimes they converge with nerves from other parts of the body and we feel the symptoms in places other than where the signal is coming from. This is called 'referred pain'

However, your back pain could be endometriosis. When tissue that acts like tissue from the uterine wall is present in other parts of the body it still responds to the hormones that control the menstrual cycle which can cause an inflammatory reaction that cycles regularly. Other signs of endometriosis include: painful intercourse, pain on going to the toilet, excessive bleeding, fatigue, and nausea. All that coincide with your menstrual cycle.

Do I need to see a doctor for my back pain?

Most of us will get back pain at some time in our lives. It is often very painful. However, it is not often dangerous, in which case, seeking pain relief and doing gentle movements can be enough. Although watching your favourite comedy or talking to your best friend may also help.

    There are a few things to look out for that mean you should seek help immediately.
  • If you begin to lose control of your bowel or bladder.
  • If you think that you have fractured part of your spine. This normally only happens in response to a trauma.
  • If you have reason to suspect it might be cancer. Most usually in older people how have previously had cancer somewhere else.
  • If you feel unwell and have a fever or chills

Is my back pain arthritis?

The word arthritis comes from the Greek for swollen joint. There are various processes that can cause swollen joints. The ones you will hear most are osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis.

Osteoarthritis is when the cartilage, which is the slippery surface of the bones in your joints, does not replenish itself as well as it used to. Osteoarthritis can cause pain and stiffness especially in the morning, but does not cause the joint to become hot and red. The best thing for osteoarthritis is movement. Start slowly and build up over time. If you would like guidance get in touch.

Rheumatoid arthritis is a body wide disorder, which can cause swelling in your joints as well as other symptoms including fever and fatigue. Most commonly it affects your hands, wrists, and feet although it can affect the spine. The pain is described as throbbing or aching and is often worse first thing in the morning. Rheumatoid arthritis is managed and slowed by medication. If you think you have rheumatoid arthritis speak to your doctor.

What to do at home if you have back pain. The general advice is to keep moving and go about your day as much as you can. If pain is preventing you from doing this then cut back to what you can manage. Initially this may mean staying at home. Back pain can be very painful and last for weeks. While you are recovering try and find ways to move your body that donâ€™t aggravate your symptoms and do them as much as you can.

Why do I have back pain when breathing in.

Two main things happen when we breath. The diaphragm contracts and pushes down into our abdomen and our ribs flare outwards and upwards slightly. Both of these things increase the space in our chest to bring air into our lungs. The ribs come from the spine to the breast bone. All the way for the lower middle part of our back up to the bottom of our neck. If the pain you are feeling is in that area and also hurts when you cough, laugh or sneeze it might be a rib that is affected.

The diaphragm is a broad flat muscle that divides the chest area that contains our heart and lungs from the abdomen that has all our organs of digestion in. As we breathe in, the pressure in the abdomen increases. If there is an issue with one of your abdominal organs breathing may aggravate it. Mostly we feel issues with our abdominal organs as an ill defined pain at the front of our bodies. However, as the nerves that supply the organs come from the back sometimes we can feel symptoms there.
